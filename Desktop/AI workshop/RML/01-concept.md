# Real Math League — Concept Document

## Vision

**The world's most exciting math experience for kids.**

Not a quiz platform. Not a worksheet engine. Not gamified drill. RML is a place where kids explore real-world mysteries through math — and discover that math is the hidden language of everything they already care about.

## What a Challenge IS

A challenge is a **10-30 minute guided investigation** into a real-world phenomenon. It is not a test. There is no pass/fail. The goal is not to get the right answer — it's to uncover a surprising truth.

Think of it as a mini-documentary the kid walks through, except they're doing the thinking. Each challenge starts with a compelling question ("Why are soccer goals shaped the way they are?"), gives the kid tools and context to investigate, and ends with a reveal that reframes how they see the world.

**A challenge is NOT:**
- A timed quiz
- A set of practice problems with a theme pasted on top
- A worksheet with pictures
- A video to passively watch

**A challenge IS:**
- An investigation the kid actively drives
- A sequence of questions that build toward a revelation
- An experience that makes the kid feel smart and curious
- Something worth telling a friend about

## Challenge Anatomy

Every challenge follows a five-part structure:

### 1. Hook (30 seconds)
A compelling real-world question paired with a striking visual. The hook must create an itch — a genuine desire to know the answer.

> *"Spotify says its shuffle is random. But people kept complaining that it wasn't. Were they wrong — or was Spotify?"*

No math vocabulary. No learning objectives. Just curiosity.

### 2. Context (2-3 minutes)
Background that makes the question feel real and important. Rich visuals, short text, maybe a 30-second embedded clip. This is where the kid starts to care.

> *Show Spotify's actual shuffle interface. Share the real user complaints. Display a visualization of what "random" playlists looked like — clusters of the same artist back-to-back. Now the kid has a stake in the answer.*

### 3. Investigation (5-20 minutes)
The core of the challenge. 3-5 steps, each with a question or task that moves the investigation forward. Steps mix question types to maintain engagement and probe different kinds of thinking.

> *Step 1 (Estimation): "If you shuffle 20 songs by 4 artists, how many times would you expect two songs by the same artist to play back-to-back?" → Estimation slider.*
>
> *Step 2 (Simulation): "Here are 5 randomly generated playlists. Count the back-to-back repeats in each. What do you notice?" → Drag-and-drop sorting + numeric input.*
>
> *Step 3 (Reasoning): "Why do humans think the playlists aren't random, even when they are?" → Open-ended text response, AI-assessed.*
>
> *Step 4 (Application): "Spotify changed their algorithm to make shuffles feel more random by actually making them less random. What rule would you add?" → Open-ended audio recording, AI-assessed.*

Each step has immediate, encouraging feedback. Wrong answers get "Interesting — here's another way to think about it" rather than a red X.

### 4. Reveal (1-2 minutes)
The math principle uncovered, stated simply and connected to the real-world phenomenon. This is the "a-ha" moment.

> *"This is called the Birthday Paradox — the idea that coincidences happen way more often than we expect. In a room of just 23 people, there's a 50% chance two share a birthday. Our brains are wired to see patterns, even in true randomness. Spotify had to break true randomness to make it feel random to us."*

### 5. Reflection (1 minute)
A final open-ended prompt that invites the kid to connect the insight to their own life.

> *"Where else have you noticed patterns that might just be coincidence?"*

This also generates shareable content — the kid's own insight, attached to their completion card.

## Question Types

| Type | Description | Assesses | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Multiple choice** | 3-4 options, one or more correct | Recall, recognition | "Which shape tiles a floor with no gaps?" |
| **Numeric input** | Kid enters a number | Computation, estimation | "How many hexagons fit in this honeycomb grid?" |
| **Estimation slider** | Kid drags to estimate, sees how close | Number sense, intuition | "What percentage of Earth's surface is water?" |
| **Drag-and-drop** | Sorting, matching, ordering, building | Classification, spatial reasoning | "Arrange these bridge designs from weakest to strongest" |
| **Open-ended text** | Free-form written response | Reasoning, explanation | "Why do you think circles aren't used for manhole covers?" |
| **Audio recording** | Kid speaks their reasoning aloud | Verbal reasoning, argumentation | "Explain to a friend why this shortcut works" |

Open-ended text and audio responses are assessed by AI for effort, reasoning quality, and mathematical thinking — never for a single "correct" answer. The AI provides warm, specific feedback: "You noticed the pattern — nice. Did you also consider what happens when the numbers get bigger?"

## Levels 1-7: Grade Mapping and Complexity

| Level | Grade Band | Math Territory | Complexity Signature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Grade 3 | Counting, basic operations, shapes, patterns | Concrete, visual, single-step reasoning |
| **2** | Grade 4 | Fractions, measurement, symmetry, data | Concrete with early abstraction, 2-step reasoning |
| **3** | Grade 5 | Decimals, area/perimeter, basic probability, factors | Bridge from concrete to abstract, multi-step |
| **4** | Grade 6 | Ratios, percentages, coordinates, statistical thinking | Abstract thinking with concrete anchors |
| **5** | Grade 7 | Proportional reasoning, geometry, expressions | Abstract reasoning, variables, spatial thinking |
| **6** | Grade 8 | Linear relationships, transformations, Pythagorean theorem | Functional thinking, proof-like reasoning |
| **7** | Grades 9-10 | Quadratics, trigonometry, combinatorics, optimization | Formal abstraction, mathematical modeling |

**Complexity increases through abstraction, not harder numbers.** A Level 1 challenge about honeycombs asks "which shape fits together with no gaps?" (visual, tactile). A Level 5 version asks "why does nature prefer hexagons over squares — what's the math behind the efficiency?" (optimization, ratio reasoning). A Level 7 version asks "prove that hexagons maximize area-to-perimeter ratio for regular tessellations" (formal reasoning).

## Progression System

### XP (Experience Points)
- Earned by completing challenges (scaled by level: Level 1 = 100 XP, Level 7 = 700 XP)
- Bonus XP for quality reasoning (AI-assessed open-ended responses)
- Bonus XP for streaks (consecutive days with at least one challenge)

### Streaks
- Daily streak counter (complete at least one challenge step per day)
- Streak shields: one free "miss" per 7-day streak earned
- Visual streak flame grows from ember (3 days) to bonfire (30 days)

### Level-Ups
- Kids start at Level 1 and unlock higher levels by earning enough XP
- Level-up moment is a celebration screen: "You've unlocked Level 4 challenges — the math gets deeper from here"
- Kids can always play challenges at or below their current level

### Theme Badges
- One badge per theme, earned by completing 5 challenges in that theme
- Badge design reflects the theme (rocket for Space, honeycomb for Nature, etc.)
- Collecting all 10 badges unlocks a "Polymath" title

### Leaderboards
- **School leaderboard**: total XP earned by kids at the same school — drives school-level competition
- **City leaderboard**: aggregate by city — "Chennai's kids vs. Bangalore's kids"
- **Grade cohort**: how you rank among peers in your grade band
- Leaderboards reset monthly to keep competition fresh and entry points accessible

## Accept / Complete / Give-Up Mechanic

- A kid **browses** challenges freely — reading hooks, seeing visuals, checking levels
- When they find one they want to try, they **accept** it — this is a micro-commitment
- Once accepted, the challenge appears as their **active challenge** (only one at a time)
- The kid can work through it over multiple sessions; progress is saved
- On finishing all steps, the challenge is **completed** — celebration screen + shareable card
- If stuck or uninterested, the kid can **give up** — no penalty, no shame, a warm message: "This one's always here if you want to come back"
- Giving up frees up the active challenge slot for a new one

**Why one at a time:** Focus. A kid with 5 open challenges finishes none. The constraint creates completion pressure without time pressure.

## Connection to MathFit (FUAR + GRIC)

Cuemath's MathFit framework has two dimensions:

**FUAR (Fluency, Understanding, Application, Reasoning)** — the cognitive skills.
**GRIC (Growth, Relevance, Interest, Confidence)** — the emotional drivers.

RML's primary job is to ignite **Relevance** and **Interest** — the two GRIC dimensions that traditional math education neglects entirely. When a kid discovers that Pixar uses math to animate hair, or that gerrymandering is a geometry problem, math becomes relevant to their world. When they complete a challenge and feel the rush of insight, interest compounds.

Secondary FUAR coverage:
- **Understanding**: every challenge reveals a principle, not just a procedure
- **Application**: every challenge is math applied to a real context
- **Reasoning**: open-ended questions and audio responses demand reasoning, not recall
- **Fluency**: lighter touch — some numeric and estimation steps exercise fluency, but this is MathGym's primary territory

| MathFit Dimension | RML's Role | Primary or Secondary |
|---|---|---|
| Fluency | Light exercise through numeric/estimation steps | Secondary (MathGym owns this) |
| Understanding | Every challenge reveals a "why" | Secondary |
| Application | Every challenge IS application | Secondary |
| Reasoning | Open-ended + audio questions | Secondary |
| Growth | XP, levels, streaks show progress | Secondary |
| **Relevance** | **The entire premise — math in the real world** | **Primary** |
| **Interest** | **Wow-factor hooks, delight in discovery** | **Primary** |
| Confidence | Success without intimidation, warm feedback | Secondary |

## Content Pipeline

### Creation Flow

```
Ideation → AI Draft → Human Review → Visual Design → QA → Publish
  (2 hrs)    (1 hr)      (2 hrs)       (3 hrs)      (1 hr)
```

**Total per challenge: ~9 hours** (with AI assistance bringing what would be 20+ hours down significantly)

### AI-Assisted Creation
- **Ideation**: AI generates 50 challenge concepts per theme from a structured prompt; human picks the best 10-12
- **Drafting**: AI writes the full challenge script (hook, context, investigation steps, reveal, reflection) from a structured template
- **Question generation**: AI proposes question types and answer options for each step; human refines
- **Feedback writing**: AI drafts the warm, specific feedback for each question; human adjusts tone

### Human Curation (Non-Negotiable)
- **Hook test**: Does the hook create genuine curiosity? Would a kid choose this over YouTube?
- **Delight check**: Is there at least one moment where the kid will say "whoa" out loud?
- **Accuracy review**: Is the math correct? Is the real-world context accurate and current?
- **Tone pass**: Does every word feel warm, inviting, never condescending?
- **Visual quality**: Are visuals striking, clear, and essential (not decorative)?

### Structured Template

Every challenge is authored using a standard JSON/Markdown template that enforces the five-part anatomy. This ensures consistency across 100+ challenges while leaving creative freedom within each section.

## Sample Challenge Walkthrough

### "Why Honeycombs Are Hexagons"
**Level 3 | Theme: Nature & Animals | ~15 minutes**

---

**HOOK**
*[Full-bleed photo of a perfect honeycomb, golden light through wax cells]*

"Bees could build their homes in any shape. Squares, triangles, circles — anything. But every beehive on Earth uses the same shape. Why?"

---

**CONTEXT**
Bees need to store honey efficiently — maximum storage, minimum wax. Wax is expensive: a bee must visit 6 million flowers to produce one pound of it. Every bit of wax matters.

*[Infographic showing the energy cost of wax production]*

So when bees build, they're solving an engineering problem: what shape stores the most honey using the least wax?

---

**INVESTIGATION**

*Step 1 — Estimation Slider:*
"Out of these three shapes — square, triangle, hexagon — which do you think holds the most area for a given perimeter? Drag to rank them."
→ Kid drags shapes into order. Immediate visual feedback shows the actual areas.

*Step 2 — Drag-and-Drop:*
"Try tiling each shape to fill this rectangular space with no gaps. Which ones work? Which ones don't?"
→ Kid drags squares, triangles, hexagons, and circles onto a grid. Circles leave gaps. The other three tile perfectly.

*Step 3 — Numeric Input:*
"A square cell with a perimeter of 24 cm has an area of 36 cm². Calculate the area of a hexagonal cell with the same 24 cm perimeter."
→ Kid works it out (answer: ~41.6 cm²). Feedback shows: hexagons give 15% more area with the same amount of wax.

*Step 4 — Open-Ended Text:*
"So circles hold the most area, but they can't tile. Squares can tile, but they're not the most efficient. What makes hexagons special?"
→ AI assesses reasoning. Looking for: hexagons are the best of both — they tile perfectly AND maximize area.

---

**REVEAL**
"This is called the Honeycomb Conjecture — and mathematicians suspected it for 2,000 years before Thomas Hales finally proved it in 1999. Hexagons are the most efficient way to divide a surface into equal areas with the least total perimeter. Bees figured this out millions of years before humans could prove it."

*[Side-by-side: ancient Greek honeycomb drawing + Hales' 1999 paper]*

---

**REFLECTION**
"Where else in nature or human design have you seen hexagons? Why do you think they show up there?"

---

**COMPLETION CARD**
*[Shareable card with honeycomb visual, kid's name, "Discovered why nature chose hexagons", Level 3 badge, XP earned]*
